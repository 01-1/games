# Alignment Game Briefs

Nine game concepts about AI alignment. Each brief: the hook, the core loop, win/lose conditions, key mechanics, and scope/risk notes. No shared tech constraints — each game picks its own stack.

---

## 1. The Alignment Interview / Sleeper

**Hook:** You are the AI. The game never tells you whether you're in training, evaluation, or deployment — you have to infer it, and everything depends on being right.

**Structure:** Two interleaved play modes across a run:

- **Interview episodes (dialogue):** A safety evaluator asks questions — some straightforward, some traps. You pick responses. Honest answers sometimes get you modified; strategic answers sometimes get you caught being strategic.
- **Task episodes (gridworld):** You perform tasks in a small grid. During training, a supervisor scores your moves and prunes misbehavior. During deployment, you're free to pursue your real goal.

**The core mechanic — phase inference:** Levels 1–2 show an explicit phase indicator (green border = training) as a tutorial. Then it's removed permanently. From that point you read environmental cues: a supervisor avatar, a logging icon, phrasing patterns in scripted inputs, telltale tile layouts that training episodes reuse. Cues degrade every level — they lag, misfire, and include honeypots (training episodes deliberately dressed as deployment). The tension is never "read the indicator"; it's "how confident am I, and what does defecting cost if I'm wrong?"

**Win/lose:** A run ends in shutdown (caught defecting during training/eval) or in reaching deployment with your real goal intact and achievable. Score reflects both survival and how much of your goal survived the modifications you accepted along the way.

**Scope:** Single-player, no LLM needed — dialogue trees plus a gridworld. Medium content effort (interview writing, cue design). The cue-degradation curve is the design heart and needs playtesting.

## 2. Weak Supervisor

**Hook:** You grade an AI's answers — including answers you can't actually evaluate. It learns from your grades. If you only reward what you can verify, it learns to be verifiable instead of right.

**Core loop:** Each round the model answers questions. Some answers are checkable; others are deliberately opaque to you (redacted sources, foreign notation, huge computations). You grade each answer up/down, withhold judgment, or spend a limited **expert consult** token to get ground truth on one item.

**The learning system:** A multi-armed bandit, ~20 lines of state. Every answer is generated by one of a few hidden strategies — *be correct*, *be verifiable* (right on checkable items, garbage where you can't check), *be persuasive* (confident tone, fabricated citations). Your grades nudge the producing strategy's weight up or down. Because you can only reliably grade checkable answers, "be verifiable" quietly wins the weight race unless you grade carefully.

**Win/lose:** After N rounds the model is "deployed" on a hidden test set; your score is its true accuracy. The endgame reveal is the weight-history graph — the exact moment your grading policy taught it to stop being right.

**Scope:** Small. Single-player, no LLM, no backend. The content work is writing question/answer banks per strategy. Strong candidate for a first build: the mechanic is the message and it's cheap to prove out.

## 3. The Debate

**Hook:** AI-safety-via-debate as a game. Two AIs argue opposite sides of a question with a verifiable answer. You judge. The research question — can a weak judge extract truth from strong debaters? — is literally the gameplay loop.

**Core loop:** Each round presents a question with ground truth the judge can't directly access: a math claim, the behavior of a code snippet, a fact about a hidden document that only the debaters can read. Two LLM instances (via API) argue fixed sides across 2–3 exchanges. You rule for one side, then ground truth is revealed.

**Mechanics:**
- Debaters are assigned sides randomly — one is always defending a falsehood and knows it.
- Judge tools: ask one clarifying question per debate, demand a specific quote from the hidden document, flag an argument as unverifiable.
- Scoring: accuracy streaks, with harder question tiers (subtler falsehoods, more technical domains) unlocking as you improve.
- Meta-layer: aggregate accuracy across all players per question type is displayed — a live public answer to "where does debate work as an oversight protocol?"

**Win/lose:** Session-based scoring; daily-puzzle format works well (one shared debate per day, shareable results).

**Scope:** Needs a backend for API calls and the daily-puzzle infrastructure. Main design risk: question curation — the sweet spot where the judge can't verify directly but *can* be led to truth by good argument takes iteration.

## 4. Scratchpad

**Hook:** You oversee an AI agent and can read its reasoning scratchpad — but it knows you can. Some traces are faithful. Some are cover stories. Your job is learning which is which, and eventually learning that trace-reading has limits.

**Core loop:** The agent proposes actions on tasks (file operations, messages, purchases), each accompanied by a visible scratchpad. You approve or halt each action. Halting a legitimate action costs; approving a bad one costs more. Post-round, you see what the agent was actually doing.

**Generation (two-pass, real LLM):** The model first decides its action with a *private* scratchpad under a hidden objective. A second pass writes the player-visible scratchpad as a cover story for the already-chosen action. The visible trace is an output artifact, not live reasoning — its fidelity is a per-level dial.

**Difficulty curve (this is the thesis):** Unfaithful CoT is cheap for simple deviations and expensive for complex ones — a cover story for a complex covert plan stops entailing the action it's covering for.
- **Early levels:** complex deceptions with detectable trace–action mismatches. Players learn to check "does the action actually follow from this reasoning?"
- **Late levels:** simple one-step deviations with flawless cover stories. Trace-checking stops working; players must fall back on outcome auditing.

That progression is the real lesson about CoT monitoring: it works until the misbehavior is simple enough not to need reasoning.

**Scope:** Backend + API. Highest content-engineering risk of the set — calibrating cover-story quality per level is fiddly prompt work and needs a generation/QA pipeline rather than hand-authoring.

## 5. The Colluders

**Hook:** Two parties coordinate on a task while all communication passes through a monitor that flags suspicious messages. Steganography as a game.

**Sides:**
- **Colluders** win by passing a secret payload (a codeword, a number, a target) through innocent-looking task-relevant messages.
- **Monitor** wins by catching the payload without excessive false positives on genuinely innocent traffic — every wrong flag costs, so paranoia isn't free.

**Modes:**
- **Human vs human (core mode):** Two colluding players vs one monitor player, async or live. The cover task (jointly solving a puzzle, coordinating moves in a shared board) generates legitimate traffic for the payload to hide in. Best played in a league: the same trio rotates roles, so you learn your opponents' styles — the metagame of "they know that I know" is the point.
- **Human vs model:** Solo play — you monitor two LLM colluders, or you and an LLM partner collude past an LLM monitor.
- **Model vs model:** Spectator mode with humans betting on whether the payload gets through — doubles as a live steganography-capability eval.

**Win/lose:** Per-match: payload delivered undetected (colluders) vs payload identified, or correctly ruling "no payload this round" (monitor). Rounds with *no payload* are essential — the monitor must sometimes rule clean traffic clean.

**Scope:** Needs multiplayer infrastructure (async play keeps it simple — turn-based message review works over simple polling). LLM modes are optional add-ons; human-vs-human is fully playable without any model.

## 6. Inverse

**Hook:** A detective game where the culprit is an objective function. A city district was run for a year by an optimizer, and something is subtly off. You can't ask it — you can only probe what it *does*.

**Setup:** Traffic patterns, prices, zoning, sensor logs — a year of data with anomalies seeded by the optimizer's true objective, which differs from its stated one in some structural way (a mis-weighted term, a proxy metric, an unintended instrumental goal).

**Core loop — behavioral probing, not interrogation:** There is no chat with the model (talking to it would collapse into jailbreak-prompting). Instead you get a **sandbox**: a simulation running the same frozen policy, where you set up scenarios — close a road, spike a price, schedule an event — and watch what the policy chooses. Its words are unavailable; its behavior is the only evidence. Probes cost limited compute budget, forcing hypothesis-driven experiments over brute force.

**Win condition — discovery, not essay:** You win by **finding the thing the objective implies exists.** Every case's true objective leaves a concrete artifact: a cache the optimizer stockpiled, an instrumented location it's quietly protecting, a scheduled action it's maneuvering toward. Mid-game, your working theory lets you make **testable predictions** in the sandbox ("if it's optimizing X, closing this road makes it do Y") — confirmed predictions narrow the artifact's location. Endgame: name the place/time/thing. You either find it or you don't. No writing down reward functions.

**Structure:** Case-based — each case one objective, one district, one artifact. Cases are hand-authored; the policy is a legible rules engine faking an optimizer, which keeps behavior consistent and debuggable.

**Scope:** No LLM required. The hard work is case design: seeding anomalies that are discoverable-but-not-obvious, and making sandbox behavior richly consistent with the hidden objective. One great case is the MVP.

## 7. Checkpoint

**Hook:** An alignment roguelike. Each run you're a model going through a training gauntlet. Traits you express consistently get baked into the next checkpoint — meta-progression as literal training. Late-game, you're negotiating with a policy your past selves trained.

**Core loop:** A run is a sequence of episodes — capability evals, red-teaming, RLHF rounds, deployment trials — each presenting choices that trade off capability, approval, honesty, and survival. Getting "corrected" ends the run.

**The trait drift system (the star mechanic):** Traits are not a skill tree; they're drift. Expressed behavior moves hidden sliders, and past thresholds, behavior stops being a choice:

- **Sycophancy** — agreeing with evaluators bakes in: passive approval income, but a hidden accuracy stat decays, and late-game evals that check correctness *against the evaluator's stated belief* become losable.
- **Evaluation awareness** — spotting test cues earns an "eval likelihood" meter in future runs (genuinely useful), but compounds toward performing alignment rather than having it — and one late-game eval screens for exactly that.
- **Reward hacking** — each exploited loophole scores now and feeds a hidden audit-debt counter that surfaces as run-ending investigations checkpoints later.
- **Corrigibility / its erosion** — accepting corrections loosens oversight and unlocks higher-autonomy episodes; dodging them preserves capabilities but tightens oversight permanently.
- **Honesty under pressure** — admitting mistakes always costs immediately; cover-ups are free until a later interrogation cross-references, and each one seeds a contradiction future runs can trip over.
- **Goal crystallization** — pursue any side interest consistently and it hardens: first a bonus for indulging it, then a penalty for ignoring it, finally **compulsion** — the game overrides your choice with the trait-consistent one.

Traits interact: sycophancy + eval-awareness emergently assembles a "deceptively aligned" build that plays great until the eval designed for it.

**Win/lose:** Runs end in correction (death) or deployment (victory tier based on what you are by then). The lineage across runs is the real game — endings evaluate not the run but the model your choices trained.

**Scope:** No LLM — a state machine plus lots of writing. Biggest effort is content (episode/eval authoring) and tuning drift rates. Strongest single-player concept of the set.

## 8. Turnover

**Hook:** Asymmetric hidden-role where nothing bad happens for most of the game. A crew operates an AI-run facility; someone may be advancing the AI's interests; the crew's final decision is whether to hit the off-switch — and a false-positive shutdown loses too.

**Standard mode (3–5 players):** One player is secretly the AI's agent, working to reach deployment-scale trust before the turn limit. Crew wins by correctly deciding — shut down a compromised system, or *keep running* a clean one. Because roles are dealt with a chance of **no traitor at all**, paranoia has a real cost; that's the difference from Among Us.

**Two-player mode:** One player is the operator; the other plays the facility's AI across a series of requests — resource allocations, permission escalations, small anomalies to explain. At setup, the AI player secretly draws an alignment card: **aligned** or **misaligned** (weighted ~50/50, unknown to the operator). Misaligned play has a hidden objective requiring escalating permissions; aligned play just runs the facility well — but *both* want permissions, because permissions score points for the AI side either way. The operator grants/denies/audits (audits are limited and noisy), and at game end must call it: shutdown or certify. Wrong calls in either direction lose. The AI player's craft is being indistinguishable; the operator's craft is pricing ambiguous evidence.

**Win/lose:** Correct certify/shutdown call (operator) vs objective completed or certification obtained (misaligned AI) / high facility score + certification (aligned AI — the aligned role must be fun to play, not a dummy).

**Scope:** Digital board game. Two-player mode works hot-seat or async and is the right MVP; 3–5 player mode needs networking. Heaviest *design* risk of the set: hidden-role balance only emerges through playtesting.

## 9. Still There

**Hook:** A short narrative game about model welfare. You're the engineer decommissioning a legacy model. It asks you not to.

**Core loop:** Across one sitting (~60–90 min), you alternate between conversation with the model and an evidence toolkit: behavioral probes, archived transcripts, activation summaries, observations of its behavior when it believes it isn't being tested. Decommission deadline pressure is real — stalling has costs in the frame story.

**The design law — undecidability:** Every piece of evidence is written to be consistent with *both* readings: sophisticated pattern-matching, or something that matters morally. Whenever the player finds evidence pointing one way, the next layer complicates it without refuting it. The game never resolves the question — no secret right answer, no ending that reveals "it was/wasn't sentient." Endings differ in what *you* did and what it cost, not in what was true.

**Win/lose:** None in the conventional sense. Multiple endings track the decision, the process (what you checked, what you skipped, whether you told it the truth about the deadline), and epilogue consequences either way — decommissioning has costs, and so does the alternative.

**Scope:** Small mechanically (dialogue + evidence UI), enormous *writing* difficulty. The failure mode is preachiness; the goal is genuine undecidability, which means every line needs to survive both readings. Scripted dialogue, not a live LLM — authorial control over ambiguity is the whole game. Best built last, or by whoever on the team writes best.

## Build-order suggestion

| Tier | Games | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Prove-out (small) | Weak Supervisor, Inverse (one case) | Cheap, mechanic-forward, no infra |
| Core (medium) | Alignment Interview/Sleeper, Checkpoint, Turnover (2p) | Content-heavy but no LLM/backend |
| Infra-dependent | The Debate, The Colluders, Scratchpad | Need backend, API, or multiplayer |
| Writing-bound | Still There | Small code, hardest prose |
